How Getty turns interaction into connection
Getty created Sculpting Harmony by blending architech, storytelling and technology with the aims to bring the creative journey of Frank Gehry and his team - who designed the Walt Disney Concert Hall - to the audience.
Opening the website, my eyes were immediately drawn to the orange background, the sketch and title's animation. The main objective of the website wasn't clearly indicated at the start but the combination of the animated sketch, the bright background color and the eye-catching title implied that this website was developed with lots of effort and thoughtfulness from the creators, with which I can expect this to be a pleasant experience to enjoy. Indeed, after understanding about its theme, this design choice has successfully enhanced the artistic values of the project, when the creative process of classical art was expressed through a digital concept.
Sculpting Harmony is designed to/The experience or Getty aims to show/archive the creative process of how Frank Gehry designed the Walt Disney Concert Hall, by means of which to honor architect as a form of art and bring these artistic values closer to the public. Through the story, the website reveals the connection between architect, music, culture with the audience. Rather than regular presentation of information, Getty provides users the ability to explore as if they were physically presenting at the building. By using different types of materials with a visually stunning design and animation, Getty suggests that the website is not only designed to educate but also to evoke the sense of creativity, which is also what Frank Gehry wanted to convey through his work.
After landing on the homepage, I followed the guide on the mouse cursor and enabled sound by clicking. My attention was drawn to the compelling homepage so I hovered the mouse cursor everywhere and explored all the animation in the homepage. Then I started scrolling down for further exploration. At the introduction part, I kept scrolling back and forth because I was fond of the parallax animation. Afterward, I scrolled down to go into chapter 1. My curiosity made me click onto the “EXPLORE THE MODEL” button for further information after finishing the video. Subsequently, I kept scrolling for next chapter, reading information, watching videos with subtitle, enjoying the animation. The process repeated a few times before I finished the experience at the website's footer.
“Chapter 1: Ship in the Box” was the part that took most of my time during the experience. The transition from the image in the parallax animation to the chapter's title is very impressive. Since it's the opening of the story, chapter 1 showed Frank's initial insight and approach towards Walt Disney Concert Hall. The interactive storytelling communicated clearly Frank's ideation about how he could enhance the connection between people and music in the design. In terms of users' experience, the inclusion of video, sketches, models and especially music prompted the feeling of being at a physical exhibition where they can sense Frank's appreciation for art and people. Furthermore, there were subtitles placed in the perfect position out of the whole layout when watching videos, making the chapter more engaging toward users whose first language is not English.
Throughout the 2-minute of interacting with the experience, my most regular action was scrolling, which is the main pattern of behavior for this website, randomly interrupted by some clicks to extend the content. I found scrolling in this website highly satisfying because of the integrated animation and transition from part to part. For example, in the Introduction the parallax-style animated footage, which created a illusion of spatial depth, got me scrolling back and forth to see the transition more clearly. Getty has skillfully turned a simple flow of action, together with the chapter-based structure, into a captivating behavior, which intensified users' immersive experience.
The seamless combination of visual design, parallax animation and the systematic structure of the narrative is what I enjoy the most about this experience. Personally, I always pay attention to the motion and transition the most when having interactive experience. Up to my expectation when I first landed on Getty's website, the parallax animation elicits an illusion of spatial depth for the whole “online space” - especially in the Introduction prologue and when transitioning among chapters - making the basic scrolling behavior more satisfying. The visually aesthetic design perfectly matches the atmosphere of the Walt Disney Concert Hall. These two factors in combination with a story layered in particular details have produced a consistent feeling of connection that can reach a wide range of audience. Notably, this consonance is also what Frank Gehry and his team wanted to convey when they started the project.
Getty's website has successfully communicated the primary message through a thoroughly arranged combination of design, storytelling and interactive experience. The website makes it easier for users to expose to information while still keep its educational quality thanks to a variation of materials in different forms. The Introduction part is an excellent opening for the whole story with its design, animation and spatial illusion, which provoke intriguing emotion from users. Each chapters are well built in solid structure of text so that users can follow in a relaxing way. The chapter to chapter organization also covers the creative process in every respect. Music is the perfect element to provoke users' feeling and set an artistic tone for the whole experience especially right from the beginning. Getty has efficiently created a connection between Frank Gehry's work and the viewers' feeling, reinforcing the work's aesthetic and artistic values.
For me, Getty's website is designed to be explored in one sitting but also invite viewers to comeback for a deeper dive. On the first visit the content is displayed enough for a short focused session. At the same time, there are small buttons such as “EXPLORE THE MODEL” or “READ MORE” which are a subtle indication for a exploration into the beneath layers. The clearly straightforward structure of chapter to chapter enable users to jump to a specific part or save it for their next visit. The parallax animation and transition make it look like the story is revealing each of its layers seamlessly, making the interaction playful and worth exploring rather than just reading a set of information. Scrolling, which is the main interactive behavior, encourages a continuous flow of action from users but also requires attention to details. This behavior doesn't just drive viewers forward but also stimulate the sense of engagement, proposing a revisit for a deeper and slower dive into the archive and the story underneath. With this design, the experience can be optimized for multiple purposes such as casual visit, education or architect/art enthusiast.
Getty included a wide range of materials such as photographs, video, music, models and hand sketch. This inclusion of diverse materials' forms relates to the approach of a documentary film or a physical exhibition in a museum, where users are presented to materials in various forms and they can explore freely, enhancing the emotional value of the story. The graphic design look and titles' typography also recall to a colorful printed art book. Meanwhile, the animation creates a impression of spatial depth, which bears a resemblance to the cinematic shots in movies. All of these methods show the significant interest and appreciation to the project from the creators themselves.
However, I found some frustrations in my first time experiencing. It was the limited control over the videos. When watching video, users can jump to a specific time part, pause or even skip the whole video. However, they can't go a few seconds forward or backward. For example, if I accidently miss a footage or just simply want to go 2 seconds backward to carefully look into some small details, I have to jump to the previous time stamp and watch the whole previous part, which is a bit time-consuming and inconvenient. Furthermore, the transparent navigation bar may be not immediately noticeable. This small issue might make it harder for first-time experience if the reader want to jump to a specific part, which is what I encountered in my first visit.
Getty's website still communicates its encouragement for repeated engagement to users through its design without any obvious mechanism or features. The chapter-based structure goes with a story with multiple layers, suggesting that viewers can freely explore as how they want to. There are button such as “READ MORE” or “EXPLORE THE MODEL” which also communicates subtle signals about a revisit for deeper discovery. The vertical format and scrolling behavior are designed for gaining enough information in one sitting, which in addition with the story's layers beneath intrigues curiosity and implies an invitation for future revisit. The insertion of music and archival materials also prompts a sense of physical presence in a museum. Through all of these organization, the website indirectly suggests that it's a open resource encouraging future returns if users want to explore more deeply.
The experience has set a formal atmosphere right from the beginning when classical music is enabled. Afterward, the archival items and story layers are designed to prompt a mood of emotion, curiosity and admiration when going further into the exploration, which is similar to how users feel when visiting a museum. With that, Getty successfully encourages a sense of appreciation and gives users a more interesting and purposeful interaction rather than just purely educational experience.
This approach of Getty suggest a slow and attentive experience, which is similar to approaching a documentary film or visiting a museum. Users are encourage to be observant of details rather than immediately rushing to the end. Furthermore, the use of classical music evoke a relaxing atmosphere, helping viewers to make connection with the materials and enjoy to the fullest. Generally, the experience indicate a highly cinematic quality and that it's designed to be savored.
